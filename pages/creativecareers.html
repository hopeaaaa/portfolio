Context

Background

The Career Development + WIL Office provides career advising and career-related programming for students and alumni.

Problem

Valuable career resources were siloed across multiple university departments and even when students could find them, career information for creative fields can be hard to navigate and connect with.

Opportunity

Centralize the information in a comprehensive hub, design three interactive tools to address the most common challenges students bring to advising, and build a visual language that makes it all feel approachable and relevant to the students using it.

Advisors noticed a pattern: students were coming into advisory meetings unprepared, not because they weren't motivated, but because they didn't know how to prepare. Emailed instructions were not enough.

The Resource Hub

The final design of the resource hub is organized around how students think. We structured everything around the questions students ask, using plain language in the copy. Advisors helped surface the most common questions and those became the architecture.

The Tools

The office knew they needed three interactive tools, one for each recurring challenge students brought to appointments.

Visual metaphor: magnifying glass

Resume Builder

The Resume Builder helps students build a master resume — a comprehensive document they can draw from when tailoring job applications.

The design is a form with illustrations to communicate the value of inputting the information. The closet analogy came from something an advisor said in a meeting. When we needed a way to explain what a master resume actually is, I drew it: your master resume is a closet, the resumes you send to employers are the outfits.

Skill Identifier

Students often struggle to identify and name their transferable skills. The tool needed to show students the many hard and soft skills associated with their education. The design starts broad by asking for your degree, and narrows toward more specific and personal content: your actual skills.

"What are your skills?" is an intimidating question. Instead of starting there, the design starts with users selecting their degree and major, then the specific software tools associated with their coursework. From these concrete selections, the associated hard and soft skills are surfaced.

Every software tool, hard skill, and soft skill is mapped to a major and lives in a spreadsheet built collaboratively with the student team.

Career Pathfinder

This tool took the longest to develop and was designed by me, after the students' early concepts kept producing the same result: degree name → job title. Photography → Photographer. Illustration → Illustrator. This didn't feel like a career pathfinder — it felt like a predictable list. I wanted to challenge that and present something more expansive.

The Reframe

Instead of degree → career, the tool became a self-reflection instrument. It asks about passions, skills, and workplace values, then surfaces career directions based on that combination. Your degree is one input, not the answer.

Drag and drop interaction

With each question, students drag and drop floating shapes into a holding circle — claiming their selections rather than typing or checking boxes. Each question has its own geometry, running from the drag targets back to the illustrations at the top of the page.

Results

At the end, students receive a four-page PDF worksheet with space to reflect on their passions, skills, and values. I drew from my experience teaching when I designed it — students needed something that could live beyond the screen, something to hand an advisor to help start the conversation. This tool is a starting point, not a verdict.
