Context

The UBC Pharmacists Clinic is a university-affiliated, pharmacist-led clinic. Unlike traditional pharmacies, pharmacist-led clinics dispense information instead of drugs. They help patients better understand, manage, and sometimes reduce their medications.

In 2023, a provincial initiative enabled pharmacists in British Columbia to prescribe medications for 21 minor ailments and contraceptives (MAC). For the clinic, this meant rethinking workflows across every level: how patients accessed care, how intake data was collected, how clinicians documented and billed, and how staff coordinated across systems. All of this had to happen without disrupting care for existing patients.

Approach

Requirements Gathering

Working with the clinical leadership team, I defined the requirements for integrating the new MAC services. This meant determining which ailments required in-person versus virtual care, establishing what patient data needed to be collected for charts and government billing, and clarifying how the booking logic would need to distinguish between MAC and existing appointment types. Requirements were then communicated to developers to begin scoping implementation.

Mapping

Using journey mapping and service blueprints, I documented both the frontstage patient experience and the backstage administrative and clinical processes — mapping existing booking, intake, clinical, and billing workflows to understand how patients, staff, and clinicians interacted across the service.

The Goal: Find where the new MAC services would create friction — where they conflicted with existing appointment types, introduced new routing and eligibility logic, and required different information to flow between systems than what the clinic was already set up to handle.

Design

Designed updated service logic to support MAC appointments, including:

  • How patients identify their condition
  • How appointment types are routed (virtual vs in-person)
  • How intake data flows into clinical and billing systems

Defined what information needed to be collected:

  • During intake
  • At the point of care
  • During billing

Service Rollout and Maintenance

The updated MAC service was piloted by initially offering appointments two days per week and supporting a subset of 10 out of the 21 eligible minor ailments. This phased approach allowed the clinic to test new workflows and assess capacity before expanding the service.

During this phase, I acted as the primary point of coordination between clinicians, MOAs, and developers, advocating for clinical and patient needs in technical discussions while also communicating system constraints and implementation considerations back to clinic staff. I led decision-making around service updates and workflow changes, ensuring feedback from the clinic was translated into actionable, human-centred improvements.
